Tile Installation & Wet-Area Waterproofing

We treat tile as a system: structure, substrate, setting materials, waterproofing (where applicable), and movement accommodation. Our baselines are the TCNA Handbook (2025) and applicable ANSI A108 / A118 / A136.1 standards, with movement joints planned per TCNA EJ171 based on conditions.

  • Showers, tub surrounds, and other wet areas: Waterproofing is selected and installed as a complete system (sheet or liquid-applied) that meets the appropriate ANSI requirements (commonly A118.10 for load-bearing bonded waterproof membranes). Seams, corners, penetrations, and drain connections are detailed per the manufacturer’s instructions and the specified method. Our detailed waterproofing comparison explains system selection and installation specifics.
  • Pre-slope, drainage, and “details that fail first”: We focus on transitions, corners, curbs, niches, benches, and terminations—because that’s where water finds shortcuts. Where a shower pan or wet-area method calls for a water test, we can perform a documented flood test per the selected system requirements and local inspection expectations.
  • Substrate evaluation & prep: We verify flatness for the chosen tile size, address bond-breakers and contaminants, and select underlayment/uncoupling/crack isolation approaches based on the substrate and use-case. Where crack isolation is specified, products meeting ANSI A118.12 are used as applicable.
  • Large-format tile (LFT) and plank tile: Flatness, mortar selection, trowel strategy, and technique are chosen to target proper coverage and reduce lippage risk. See our detailed guide to large-format tile installation for substrate, mortar, and technique specifications. Where appropriate, we use lippage-control systems and plan layout around focal lines and terminations—without forcing “pretty” choices that compromise function.
  • Floors (tile): We evaluate structure and deflection suitability for the finish (commonly L/360 for ceramic/porcelain, stricter criteria for natural stone depending on the assembly). Movement accommodation is planned at perimeters, changes of plane, and within fields as conditions require (EJ171 guidance).
  • Backsplashes & wall tile: Clean cuts, finished edges, and correct changes-of-plane detailing. Penetrations (outlets, valves, fixtures) are cut and sealed appropriately, and transitions are finished with compatible trim or sealant based on the application.
  • Heated floors (electric radiant): Installed per the system manufacturer’s instructions, with staged resistance testing documented where the manufacturer specifies it. Controls, sensors, and required electrical protections are coordinated with properly licensed electrical work when required.
  • Grout, sealants, and movement joints: We select grout and sealant types appropriate to the location (wet area vs. dry, chemical exposure, movement). Changes of plane and movement joints are treated as functional parts of the assembly—not optional cosmetics.

Compliance note: Where permits or inspections apply (scope-dependent), work is coordinated in line with the New Jersey Uniform Construction Code and local requirements. Plumbing/electrical/HVAC/structural scope is performed by or referred to properly licensed trades as required.

Bathroom & Kitchen Remodeling

Remodel coordination with sequencing that protects waterproofing and finishes. Scope and changes stay in writing so you don’t get surprised mid-project.

  • Bathrooms: Layout and finish planning around moisture management, serviceability, and durable transitions.
  • Kitchens: Backsplashes and floors coordinated with cabinets/counters; clean terminations and protection at edges.
  • Flooring (non-tile): LVP and engineered wood installed with substrate prep and underlayment per manufacturer requirements.
  • Finish carpentry: Baseboards, trims, and transition details that protect edges and complete the room.
  • Drywall & paint: Patch/skim and prep-focused finishing for clean final surfaces.

Note: Electrical, plumbing, HVAC, roofing, and structural work is referred to properly licensed trades as required.

Property Maintenance & Minor Repairs

Recurring service plans for routine upkeep, minor repairs, and scheduled walkthroughs. Each visit includes a simple log and photos so owners and landlords can track condition over time.

  • Seasonal walkthroughs: Visual interior/exterior checks (where safely accessible) to spot issues early.
  • Minor repairs: Door/cabinet adjustments, hardware updates, light carpentry, fixture swaps—performed to applicable requirements and manufacturer instructions.
  • Grout & caulk maintenance: Condition-based refresh in wet areas using application-appropriate sealants and prep methods.
  • Paint touch-ups: Targeted repairs with proper surface prep (best-effort blending depends on existing coatings/age).
  • Priority scheduling: Maintenance clients receive preferred windows for non-emergency requests.
  • Documentation: Photos + notes for each visit—useful for remote owners, insurers, and planning.

How the Plans Work

  1. Initial walkthrough: Joint property review, scope definition, written agreement.
  2. Scheduled visits: Monthly or seasonal cadence with clear task lists and windows.
  3. Documentation: Notes and photos after each visit for tracking and planning.
  4. Referrals: Out-of-scope issues referred to properly licensed specialists as needed.

Scope & Limitations

  • Plans cover routine upkeep and minor repairs—not emergency response or full code inspections.
  • Major plumbing, electrical, HVAC, roofing, or structural work is referred to properly licensed trades.
  • Tasks depend on safe access, weather, and site conditions; scheduling may adjust for safety/compliance.
  • Materials and third-party costs are client-supplied or pre-approved in writing.
  • Work is performed under written service agreements/work orders defining scope, pricing, and responsibilities.
