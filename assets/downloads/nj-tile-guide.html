1. Understanding New Jersey Requirements

When you're planning a bathroom remodel or tile installation in New Jersey, permits and codes matter more than you might think. Understanding what's requiredâ€”and whyâ€”protects your investment and ensures your project is done right.

When Do You Need a Permit?

Not all tile work requires a permit. Here's the general rule:

  • Permits Required: Bathroom remodels, structural changes, work involving plumbing or electrical, changes to building envelope, wet areas in kitchens
  • No Permit Needed: Cosmetic tile work on existing surfaces (replacing a kitchen backsplash), repairs using identical materials

The Uniform Construction Code (UCC)

New Jersey follows the International Building Code (IBC) through the Uniform Construction Code. This code covers everything from waterproofing requirements to substrate flatness standards. Licensed contractors follow these codes; unlicensed workers may not.

Why Permits Protect You:
  • Creates inspection checkpoints that verify quality
  • Documents work for insurance and resale
  • Ensures electrical and plumbing meet code
  • Establishes a complaint process if work is substandard

2. Waterproofing Essentials

Here's a harsh truth: 80% of shower failures trace back to waterproofing and substrate issuesâ€”not the tile itself. The work you can't see matters more than the work you can see.

Why Waterproofing Fails

Showers fail when water gets behind the tile. This happens when:

  • No waterproofing membrane is used
  • Waterproofing is improperly installed or incomplete
  • Substrate moves or cracks, breaking the seal
  • Penetrations (drains, pipes) aren't properly detailed
  • Grout fails and allows water penetration

Types of Waterproofing Systems

  • Sheet Membranes: Rolls applied directly to substrate (Schluter KERDI, Laticrete Hydro Ban Board)
  • Liquid-Applied Membranes: Brush or spray applied (RedGard, Custom products)
  • Bonded Systems: Engineered systems verified through testing
  • Traditional Systems: Hot-mopped or CPE liners (older installations)

Flood Testing

The best way to verify waterproofing before tile is applied: fill the shower pan and watch for leaks. Proper flood testing per ASTM D5957 prevents surprises after your bathroom is finished. When conditions allow, this test should be documented with photos.

3. TCNA Standards & Installation Methods

The Tile Council of North America (TCNA) publishes the Handbook for Ceramic, Glass, and Stone Tile Installation. It's the industry standard for proper tile installation in North America.

What "TCNA-Compliant" Means

When a contractor says they follow TCNA standards, they mean they:

  • Prepare substrates to specified flatness (typically 1/8" in 10')
  • Use appropriate thinset mortars for the application
  • Apply full mortar coverage (no voids)
  • Use proper grouting techniques
  • Detail transitions, thresholds, and penetrations correctly

Substrate Flatness

TCNA specifies that substrates must be flat within 1/8" over any 10-foot span, and 1/16" over any 3-foot span. Large deviations cause lippage (uneven tile surfaces), poor mortar coverage, and eventual failures.

Mortar Coverage

TCNA requires 95% mortar coverage on the back of all tiles. This means:

  • Full back-buttering for tiles over 8x8"
  • Trowel ridges must support the full tile weight
  • No voids or hollow spots

4. Substrate Preparation & Verification

Professional tile installation begins long before tile goes on the wall. Substrate preparation is the foundation of every successful project.

Substrate Types

  • Drywall: In wet areas, use cement board or waterproof drywall, never standard drywall
  • Concrete Board: Requires proper fastening and appropriate mortar
  • Uncoupling Membranes: Systems like Schluter DITRA that both waterproof and manage movement
  • Mortar Bed: Traditional sand/cement bed, requires proper slope and perimeter waterproofing

Verification Process

A professional contractor will:

  • Evaluate existing structure for flatness, moisture, and integrity
  • Test substrate with moisture meter before tile installation
  • Plan waterproofing strategy based on findings
  • Document substrate condition with photos
  • Verify flatness with straightedge before application

5. Tile Selection & Materials

Tile selection is about more than aesthetics. The right tile for your application ensures durability and performance.

Tile Categories

  • Ceramic: Fired clay, good for walls and floors, affordable
  • Porcelain: Denser than ceramic, more durable, water-resistant
  • Natural Stone: Marble, granite, slateâ€”beautiful but requires sealing and careful maintenance
  • Glass: Decorative, requires proper substrate and installation method

Slip Resistance

For bathroom floors and shower areas, choose tile with appropriate slip resistance (typically COF 0.60 or higher). Glazed tile is slippery when wet; textured or matte finishes are safer.

Large-Format Tile

Tile larger than 15" on a side requires:

  • Perfectly flat substrate (tighter tolerances)
  • Full back-buttering
  • Modified thinsets designed for large tile
  • Careful lippage control

6. Contractor Selection Checklist

Choosing the right contractor is the single most important decision in your project. Use this checklist to evaluate candidates.

Verify Licensing

  • Ask for NJ HIC number and verify at nj.gov/oag/hic
  • Check for complaints or disciplinary history
  • Confirm insurance is current and adequate

Questions to Ask

  • How long have you been in business?
  • Can you provide references from recent projects?
  • Do you pull permits and handle inspections?
  • What waterproofing systems do you use?
  • Do you provide a written contract with detailed scope?
  • What is your warranty on workmanship?
  • How do you handle unexpected issues?

Request a Written Estimate

Any estimate over $500 must include:

  • Detailed scope of work
  • Specific materials and brands
  • Timeline and payment schedule
  • Warranty terms
  • Your 3-day right of rescission notice

7. Red Flags to Avoid

Be cautious if a contractor:

  • Refuses to pull permits: Legal work requires permits; refusal is a red flag
  • No written contract: Always get everything in writing
  • Requests full payment upfront: Typical schedule is 30-50% deposit, balance on completion
  • Guarantees no waterproofing needed: Every wet area needs waterproofing
  • Cannot provide references: Ask for recent, verifiable projects
  • Pressures you to decide immediately: Good contractors are busy; rushing is suspicious
  • No liability insurance: Always verify current coverage
  • Vague about substrate prep: Proper prep should be detailed in scope

8. Your Rights Under New Jersey Law

The 3-Day Right of Rescission

Under New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act, you have 3 business days to cancel any home improvement contract over $25. Your contractor must clearly disclose this right in your contract. This is non-waivableâ€”any clause trying to remove this right is void.

What Must Be in Your Contract

  • Contractor name, address, and license number
  • Detailed description of work
  • Materials to be used (brands, specifications)
  • Total cost breakdown
  • Payment schedule
  • Timeline for completion
  • Warranty information
  • 3-day right of rescission notice

If Something Goes Wrong

  • Document everything with photos and written communication
  • Send written notice of defects within reasonable time
  • Request written response within 10 days
  • File complaint with NJ Division of Consumer Affairs if unresolved
  • Consult an attorney for significant disputes

Licensing Verification

Verify your contractor's license at: www.nj.gov/oag/hic

You can check license status, complaints, and disciplinary history. Never hire unlicensed contractors.
