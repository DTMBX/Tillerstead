Guides & Tips

Quick reference for planning your tile project.

Measuring Tips
  • Measure twice: Record length and width at multiple points—rooms aren't always perfectly square.
  • Draw it out: Sketch the room shape and note obstacles (toilets, vanities, doorways).
  • Account for alcoves: Measure recessed areas separately and add to total.
  • Wall height: For wall tile, measure floor-to-ceiling or floor-to-desired-height at multiple points.
  • Complex shapes: Break into rectangles, calculate each, then sum.
Layout & Pattern Selection
  • Straight/stacked: Lowest waste, easiest to install. Works well with square tiles.
  • 1/3 offset (recommended): Reduces lippage risk on plank tiles compared to 50% offset. Most installers prefer this for wood-look tile.
  • 50% offset (brick): Classic subway look, but can emphasize tile warpage. Best for shorter tiles (≤12").
  • Diagonal: Visually expands space but increases waste. Plan layout to minimize small corner cuts.
  • Herringbone: Dramatic pattern but highest waste (20–30%). Requires precise cuts.

Always dry-lay a section before setting to verify pattern and check for issues.

Mortar Coverage Check
  • Lift and check: Periodically lift a just-set tile to verify mortar coverage on the back.
  • Target coverage: Floor tile: 80%+ coverage. Wet areas and large-format: 95%+ coverage.
  • Full contact: No voids or hollow spots, especially at corners and edges.
  • Back-butter when needed: For large-format tile, apply a thin layer of mortar to tile back in addition to substrate.
  • Directional troweling: Comb mortar in straight lines (not swirls) for easier air release.
Movement Joints
  • Perimeter joints: Leave a gap at all walls, cabinets, and fixtures—filled with caulk, not grout.
  • Field joints: For large areas, movement joints may be needed every 20–25 feet (varies by conditions).
  • Change of plane: Where floors meet walls, use caulk at the transition.
  • Existing joints: Honor any expansion/control joints in the substrate.

Movement joints prevent cracks from building stress. They're required by industry standards for most installations.

Understanding Waste Factors
  • 10%: Minimum for straight layouts in simple rectangular rooms.
  • 12–15%: Standard for most offset/brick patterns.
  • 15–20%: Diagonal layouts, rooms with many cuts.
  • 20–30%: Herringbone, complex patterns, or rooms with many obstacles.
  • Attic stock: Always order extra (1 box or +5%) for future repairs. Dye lots vary!

When in doubt, round up. Running short mid-project is far more costly than having extra.

Substrate Preparation
  • Clean: Remove dust, debris, adhesive residue, and contaminants.
  • Flat: Check with a straightedge. For large-format tile, flatness is critical.
  • Sound: No loose areas, delamination, or structural issues.
  • Dry: Concrete must cure adequately. Test moisture if needed.
  • Primed: Many substrates need primer for proper mortar bond.

Most tile failures trace back to substrate problems. Invest time here.
